# Topic

考察 2020–2025 年间 AI agent（基于大语言模型的自主/半自主智能体）在工作场所的扩散如何影响就业形势——就业规模、结构与任务构成。以任务层面的替代与互补机制为核心，论证 AI agent 对就业的影响主要表现为任务重构而非总量层面的简单替代。

# Core Question

Why does the 2020-2025 diffusion of AI agents reshape employment primarily through task-level substitution and complementarity rather than aggregate net job destruction?

# Intended Thesis

AI agents in 2020-2025 primarily automate routine cognitive tasks while raising demand for complementary tasks of supervision, orchestration, and judgment, so their employment effect is a structural restructuring of tasks within jobs rather than aggregate net job destruction; the net effect depends on the relative pace of task automatability versus complementary-task demand.

# Paper Type

single_event_mechanism

# Scope

- Period: 2020–2025
- Region: United States and OECD high-income economies
- Actors: firms adopting AI agents, knowledge workers, AI tool providers, the labor market
- Mechanisms: task-level automation of routine cognitive tasks, complementary-task demand for supervision and orchestration, skill recomposition, within-job task restructuring

# Exclusions

- Do not extrapolate the 2020–2025 observations to all economies or to the long run (>2025) without explicit qualifiers.
- Do not give country-specific policy prescriptions.
- Do not make quantitative causal estimation claims without firm-level or occupation-level data.
- Do not treat this period's evidence as universal proof that AI necessarily causes mass unemployment, nor that AI necessarily leaves employment unaffected.

# Seed Claims

1. During 2020–2025 AI agents primarily automated routine, codifiable cognitive tasks.
2. AI agent adoption simultaneously raised demand for complementary tasks such as supervision, orchestration, and output judgment.
3. Task-level substitution and aggregate net job destruction are distinct phenomena that AI agents link but do not equate.
4. The net employment effect depends on the relative pace of task automatability versus growth in complementary-task demand.
5. Within the observation window the effect appeared more as within-job task restructuring and skill recomposition than as large aggregate net job losses.
6. Existing labor statistics struggle to capture task-level restructuring in a timely way.
