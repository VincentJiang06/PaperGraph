Title: GPTs are GPTs: An Early Look at the Labor Market Impact Potential of Large Language Models
Authors: Tyna Eloundou, Sam Manning, Pamela Mishkin, Daniel Rock (2023/2024)
URL fetched: https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.10130
(Published version: Science, 2024, doi:10.1126/science.adj0998)

VERBATIM EXTRACTS (task-level exposure / task substitution):

- "around 80% of the U.S. workforce could have at least 10% of their work tasks affected"
- "approximately 19% of workers may see at least 50% of their tasks impacted"
- With direct LLM access: "about 15% of all worker tasks in the US could be completed significantly faster" at the same level of quality
- With LLM-powered software and tooling built on top of LLMs: this increases to "between 47 and 56% of all tasks"
- Wage distribution: impacts span all income levels, with "higher-income jobs potentially facing greater exposure to LLM capabilities and LLM-powered software."

Interpretation note: These are estimates of task EXPOSURE (potential), not realized job losses. The authors argue LLMs exhibit traits of general-purpose technologies (GPTs).

SOURCE S3
Title: The Simple Macroeconomics of AI
Author: Daron Acemoglu (MIT), May 12, 2024 (prepared for Economic Policy; NBER WP 32487)
URL: https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/2024-05/The%20Simple%20Macroeconomics%20of%20AI.pdf
(Read directly from the fetched PDF, pages 1-14.)

VERBATIM EXTRACTS (abstract):

- "Using existing estimates on exposure to AI and productivity improvements at the task level, these macroeconomic effects appear nontrivial but modest—no more than a 0.66% increase in total factor productivity (TFP) over 10 years."
- "Consequently, predicted TFP gains over the next 10 years are even more modest and are predicted to be less than 0.53%."
- "Empirically, I find that AI advances are unlikely to increase inequality as much as previous automation technologies because their impact is more equally distributed across demographic groups, but there is also no evidence that AI will reduce labor income inequality. Instead, AI is predicted to widen the gap between capital and labor income."

VERBATIM EXTRACTS (task-based model, pages 2, 12):

- "Automation (or more precisely extensive-margin automation) involves AI models taking over and reducing costs in certain tasks. In the case of generative AI, various mid-level clerical functions, text summary, data classification, advanced pattern recognition, and computer vision tasks are among those that can be profitably automated."
- "Task complementarity can increase the productivity in tasks that are not fully automated and may even raise the marginal product of labor."
- On wages: "(a) automation always produces a positive effect on wages (and labor demand) because it increases productivity (or equivalently, reduces costs)... (b) simultaneously, automation displaces workers from the tasks they used to perform. The negative displacement effect is represented by the second term." "In general, this expression has ambiguous sign, so automation can increase or reduce wages."

Interpretation note: Acemoglu's model separates task-level automation (substitution) from aggregate outcomes; his headline is that near-term macro effects are MODEST, while distributional effects (capital vs labor) can be adverse.

SOURCE S4
Title: Introducing the Anthropic Economic Index (first report; based on Claude.ai / Clio usage data)
Author: Anthropic, February 2025.
URL: https://www.anthropic.com/news/the-anthropic-economic-index

VERBATIM EXTRACTS (how AI is ACTUALLY used at the task level — automation vs augmentation):

- "Overall, we saw a slight lean towards augmentation, with 57% of tasks being augmented and 43% of tasks being automated."
- Augmentation defined: "AI was not being used to replace people doing tasks, but instead worked with them, engaging in tasks like validation (e.g., double-checking the user's work), learning (e.g., helping the user acquire new knowledge and skills), and task iteration (e.g., helping the user brainstorm or otherwise doing repeated, generative tasks)."
- "Over one-third of occupations (roughly 36%) see AI use in at least a quarter of their associated tasks, while approximately 4% of occupations use it across three-quarters of their associated tasks."
- "As we predicted, there wasn't evidence in this dataset of jobs being entirely automated: instead, AI was diffused across the many tasks in the economy, having stronger impacts for some groups of tasks than others."

Interpretation note: AI adoption operates at the TASK level, not the whole-job level; augmentation modestly exceeds automation in observed usage.

SOURCE S5
Title: Canaries in the Coal Mine? Six Facts about the Recent Employment Effects of Artificial Intelligence
Authors: Erik Brynjolfsson, Bharat Chandar, Ruyu Chen (Stanford Digital Economy Lab), 2025.
URL: https://digitaleconomy.stanford.edu/publication/canaries-in-the-coal-mine-six-facts-about-the-recent-employment-effects-of-artificial-intelligence/
Data: millions of monthly ADP payroll records through July 2025.

VERBATIM EXTRACTS (realized net job change in a specific segment):

- "early-career workers (ages 22-25) in the most AI-exposed occupations have experienced a 16 percent relative decline in employment" (since late 2022)
- "employment for workers in less exposed fields and more experienced workers in the same occupations has remained stable or continued to grow"
- "adjustments occur primarily through employment rather than compensation"
- "employment declines are concentrated in occupations where AI is more likely to automate, rather than augment, human labor"
- The findings are "early, large-scale evidence consistent with the hypothesis that the AI revolution is beginning to have a significant and disproportionate impact on entry-level workers in the American labor market."

Interpretation note: This is direct evidence that task substitution has begun to translate into HEADCOUNT reductions, but only in a narrow segment (young entrants in the most exposed occupations, e.g. software, customer support).

Author: The Budget Lab at Yale (Martha Gimbel et al.), 2025.
Primary URL: https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/evaluating-impact-ai-labor-market-current-state-affairs
URL fetched (reproduction with author quotes): https://fortune.com/2026/02/02/ai-labor-market-yale-budget-lab-ai-washing/

VERBATIM EXTRACTS (aggregate / economy-wide net effect):

- "The picture of AI's impact on the labor market that emerges from our data is one that largely reflects stability, not major disruption at an economy-wide level."
- Martha Gimbel (executive director, Yale Budget Lab): "No matter which way you look at the data, at this exact moment, it just doesn't seem like there's major macroeconomic effects here."
- On the pace of change: while occupational changes have occurred since ChatGPT's late-2022 release, "the rate of change has not increased enough to signal a massive shift."

Additional (from Budget Lab methodology, widely reported): a ~33-month analysis window found "no discernible disruption" in broad employment metrics and no clear correlation between AI exposure and aggregate unemployment; the occupational mix is changing but not faster than in past technological eras.

Interpretation note: At the AGGREGATE (economy-wide) level there is no measurable net displacement to date; disruption remains concentrated and small relative to the whole labor market.
