--- VERBATIM QUOTES ---

(On the paper's core question)
"We address three core questions about the hypothesized role of newly emerging job categories ('new work') in counterbalancing the erosive effect of task-displacing automation on labor demand: what is the substantive content of new work; where does it come from; and what effect does it have on labor demand?"

(On the scale of employment accounted for by new work — confirmed consistently across NBER, BU TPRI, and RePEc mirrors)
"the majority of current employment is in new job specialties introduced after 1940"
(BU TPRI mirror renders the same finding as: "the majority of current employment is in new job specialties introduced since 1940")

(On where new work creation has moved over time)
"the locus of new work creation has shifted—from middle-paid production and clerical occupations over 1940–1980, to high-paid professional and, secondarily, low-paid services since 1980"

(On augmentation vs. automation — the key mechanism for the "does creation offset substitution" question)
"Augmentation and automation innovations have countervailing effects on labor demand: augmentation innovations boost occupational labor demand while automation innovations erode it."
"new work emerges in response to technological innovations that complement occupational outputs and demand shocks [that raise occupational demand]"

(On the recent-decades trend — directly relevant to whether creation is still offsetting substitution)
"the demand-eroding effects of automation innovations have intensified in the last four decades while the demand-increasing effects of augmentation innovations have not"

--- NOTES ---
This is the key academic reference for the "creation side" of N-0005: historically (1940-2018), most employment growth came from brand-new job titles/specialties created by technology, and augmentation innovations that raise labor demand have historically offset automation's erosive effect. BUT the paper's own finding is a caution against a simple "AI creates more than it destroys" reading going forward: the erosive (automation) channel has been intensifying over the last four decades while the offsetting (augmentation) channel has NOT kept pace. This is directly usable as counter-evidence/tension for any claim that job creation will straightforwardly offset AI-driven substitution in 2020-2025.
Note: This paper's data window ends in 2018 and is not itself AI-specific (it covers general automation/augmentation technology 1940-2018); it establishes the historical baseline mechanism, not 2020-2025 AI-specific figures.
